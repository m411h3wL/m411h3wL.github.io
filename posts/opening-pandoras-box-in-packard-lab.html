Opening Pandora's Box in Packard Lab

What started as stream of consciousness leads me to some old analysis of a beloved Lehigh professor.

June 20, 2026 7 min read

It was freshman fall 2022. I was taking CSE017 – Programming and Data Structures in Packard Lab I think it was room 416? The one where the seats face the UC and you can crack open a window, which didn’t have a screen - and sit on the ledge and look out at the church. It was my second time in that room. I’d sat there when I was a senior in high school next to my sister’s friend when I visited Lehigh.

I had already taken AP computer science classes in high school, so I had highly underestimated the difficulty of the course. It was just going to be simple Java and OOP over again. Wrong. There were lots of assignments and the goddamn ‘ZyBooks’, which I never cared to do. There was Big O notation, Time/Space Complexity, search algorithms, etc. I remember learning in lecture about how Bill Gates designed his school's scheduling software and supposedly tweaked it, so he'd be placed in classes with more girls. I was excited to take more CS classes.

December rolled around and we sat in the basement lecture hall of Packard for our final exam. I went back into my email to try and find the final exam instructions to see if they mentioned anything about AI. They didn’t. But the final line in my professor’s email made me laugh out loud.

I broke the email up for ease of reading here, but it went like:

“As a reminder, our final exam will take place from 4-7 pm today in Packard Lab 101. Please bring your computers (fully charged) … The exam will include a programming portion… short comprehension questions… Good luck as you wrap up your studying -- you are all amazing humans and can will crush this exam!” – Prof Z

As we sat down for our exams I had been hearing about a tool that could help solve the programming assignments. I had never used it for any of the coding assignments – it was enjoyable to code without it. But the final was difficult. I had decided not to study – the youthful bravado of thinking I knew everything and was the best programmer from my public high school. I raced through the comprehension questions and built two projects that passed the required tests. The next two projects were difficult, however. My code was broken, riddled with errors, and I was starting to panic.

And that, was when I opened Altman’s box. Pause. Pandora’s box. I opened Pandora’s box.

I just threw the code in, it took a while, but I was stumped anyway. 5 or 10 minutes passed, and it gave me an output. I don’t even remember what the chat box formatting looked like. I threw it in and ran the code. The terminal output was correct. I may have ran a couple of other System.out.println tests throughout the project at certain checkpoints I had struggled with, or I might’ve just instantly clicked submit, closed my laptop, checked my shoulders, the clock and then slowly opened my laptop to pretend that I was still working on it. Yeah, that’s what I probably did.

Maybe my professor was right in her cheerily emailed tagline of motivation, I was an amazing human. I was going to score highly on my first final exam.

(If you look closely at her email, she also had a typo – ‘ and can will crush this exam’. Such a typo would never exist nowadays.)

But I had cheated, right? I decided not to tell anyone about what I had done.

December passed along with its holidays, and I hadn’t thought about it very much. Despite failing to reconnect with the high school ex-girlfriend, I had spent the holidays in a good mood and excited to get back to an amazing freshmen year where I’d be rushing a fraternity I’d put in a lot of work to get connected with during the Fall.

When I returned to school to take CSE109, the school had begun issuing statements about AI. I went back through my inbox to find a February 2023 email linking to a LinkedIn post from Nathan Urban Provost and SVP at Lehigh University and Dr. Jeremey Littau’s – one of my older sister's (a Lehigh grad and journalism major)favorite professors post on substack. Sidenote: I have officially signed up for Substack now. I read through it and parts of my brain in which I’ve been actively thinking, what now seem like primitive thoughts, were inspired and brought into a colorful light by Littau’s vivid and before-their-time descriptions.

He speaks of shaping and prodding the system, like a potter shaping clay. He also theorizes that ChatGPT's "usefulness is correlated with our ability to critique it and ask it follow-up questions."

In my mind, Dr. Littau’s theory is no longer that - it is more like a fact. More on this in a later post about the differences between two family member's ChatGPT respones to identical prompts.

At the end of this piece, written in December 2022, Littau writes in a positive vein about ChatGPT. He writes that learning is asking the follow-up, not the initial question. He highlights the strength of using ChatGPT to find that initial starting point more easily. He writes that it’s a “huge opportunity to spend our brainpower on pursuing novel questions of substance and importance.”

A “jumping off point … for truth and real answers”

Although I never took a class with Dr. Littau, likely in Coppee Hall, his influence reached me all the same. While he taught Journalism across campus, an 18-year-old Matt sat in the basement of Packard Lab, unkowingly opened up a technology that I strongly believe will be correlated to the success of my life's work. I want to use this technology to elevate my present working knowledge to a point where I can ask novel questions of substance and importance as Dr. Littau writes.

P.S. I recommend reading Dr. Littau's article. The part where he shows his early prompts is pretty damn funny and insightful. I'm unsure how to find my earliest prompts because I don't want to scroll for ~4 years worth of prompts, but when I figure it out I'll link a post.

Thanks for reading,

Matt
