Opening Pandora's Box in Packard Lab

What started as stream of consciousness leads me to some old analysis of a beloved Lehigh professor.

June 20, 2026 7 min read

It was freshman fall 2022. I was taking Programming and Data Structures in Packard Lab - I think it was room 416. It was the room where the seats face the UC and you could crack open those ancient windows, which didn’t have screens - and sit on the ledge looking out at the church. It was my second time in that room. I’d sat there once before as a senior visiting my sister.

I had already taken AP Computer Science in high school, so I confidently—and incorrectly—assumed the course would be easy. It was going to be Java and object-oriented programming all over again. No problem. Wrong. There were assignments everywhere and the damn 'ZyBooks', which I never cared to do. There was Big O notation, Time/Space Complexity, search algorithms, etc. For some reason I remember learning in lecture about how Bill Gates had designed his high school's scheduling software using these concepts.He supposedly tweaked it, so he'd be placed in classes with more girls. I was excited to take more CS classes.

December rolled around and we sat in the basement lecture hall of Packard for our final exam.

I went back into my email to try and find the final exam instructions to see if they mentioned anything about AI. They didn’t. But the final line in my professor’s email made me laugh out loud. I broke the email up for ease of reading here, but it went like:

“As a reminder, our final exam will take place from 4-7 pm today in Packard Lab 101. Please bring your computers (fully charged) … The exam will include a programming portion… short comprehension questions… Good luck as you wrap up your studying -- you are all amazing humans and can will crush this exam!” – Prof

As we sat down for our exams I had been hearing about a tool that could help solve the programming assignments. I had never used it for any of the coding assignments – it was enjoyable to code without it. But the final was difficult. I had decided not to study – the youthful bravado of thinking I knew everything and was the best programmer from my public high school. I raced through the comprehension questions and built two projects that passed the required tests. The next two projects were difficult, however. My code was broken, riddled with errors, and I was starting to panic.

I looked around at other students, their hands running through their hair, also confused. And that, was when I opened Altman’s box. Pause. Pandora’s box. I opened Pandora’s box.

To be clear, the technology was so new that the professors hadn't said anything other than 'it probably wont help you'. The lines were blurred. So I opened the box and threw my code in - it took a while - but I was stumped anyway. 5 or 10 minutes passed, and it gave me an output. I threw it into VSCode and ran the file. The terminal output was correct. I may have ran a couple of other System.out.println tests throughout the project at certain checkpoints I had struggled with, or I might’ve just instantly clicked submit, closed my laptop, checked my shoulders, the clock and then slowly opened my laptop to pretend that I was still working on it. Yeah, that’s what I probably did.

As I walked out of the lecture hall, I took a sigh of relief. Maybe my professor was right in her cheerily emailed tagline of motivation, I was an amazing human. I was going to score highly on my first final exam. Little did she know that on the backend of that exam, my apparent 'amazing-ness' was far from human.

(If you look closely at her email, she also had a typo – ‘ and can will crush this exam’. Such a typo would never exist nowadays.)

I wondered if I was the only one who used it. Had I cheated or broken any explicit rules? I decided not to tell anyone about what I had done until I returned back in the Spring.

December passed along with its holidays, and I hadn’t thought about it very much. Despite failing to reconnect with the high school ex-girlfriend, I had spent the holidays in a good mood, excited to return to what had already been an incredible freshman year. I was looking forward to rushing a fraternity I'd been getting to know since my first days on campus and continuing to meet new people.

When I returned to school to take a systems software course, the school had begun issuing statements about AI. For this post, I went back through my inbox to find the exact statements. I found a February 2023 email linking to a LinkedIn post from Nathan Urban,Provost and SVP at Lehigh University, and Dr. Jeremey Littau’s – one of my older sister's (a Lehigh grad and journalism major)favorite professors - substack. Sidenote: I have officially signed up for Substack now. I read through it. I'm grateful I found it.

In his piece, he speaks of shaping and prodding the system, like a potter shaping clay. He also theorizes that ChatGPT's "usefulness is correlated with our ability to critique it and ask it follow-up questions."

It took me a while to understand the true power of this tool was in defining how you interacted with it. I learned quickly that the more context I fed it, the better answers I would recieve over time. But I wasn't aware of how much the process of building progressively stronger and more incisve prompts, was really about externalizing my own thinking.

At the end of this piece, written in December 2022, Littau writes that learning is asking the follow-up, not the initial question. He highlights the strength of using ChatGPT to find that initial starting point more easily. He writes that it’s a “huge opportunity to spend our brainpower on pursuing novel questions of substance and importance.”

... finally calling it a, “jumping off point … for truth and real answers”

I'm still developing my own working model of how to most effectively interact with this technology, but I find his framing particularly clarifying.

While he taught my sister Journalism across campus, an 18-year-old Matt, sat in the basement of Packard Lab, unkowingly opened up a Pandora's box - whose contents included a tool that may prove foundational to the future I hope to build. I want to use this technology, not to find shortcuts with blurred lines, but to elevate my present working knowledge to a point where I can ask novel questions of substance and importance, as Dr. Littau so eloquently writes.

I recommend reading Dr. Littau's article. It's insane that it was almost 4 years ago. The part where he shows his early prompts is pretty damn funny and insightful. I'm unsure how to find my early prompts because I don't want to scroll for ~4 years worth of prompts, but when I figure it out I'll link a post.

Thanks for reading,

Matt
