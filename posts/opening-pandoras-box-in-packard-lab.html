Opening Pandora's Box in Packard Lab

What started as stream of consciousness leads me to some old analysis of a beloved Lehigh professor.

June 20, 2026 7 min read

It was freshman fall 2022. I was taking CSE017 – Programming and Data Structures in Packard Lab I think it was room 416? The one where the seats face the UC and you can crack open a window, which didn’t have a screen - and sit on the ledge and look out at the church. It was my second time in that room. I’d sat there when I was a senior in high school next to my sister’s friend when I visited Lehigh.

Anyway, I had already taken AP computer science classes in high school, so I had highly underestimated the difficulty of the course. It was just going to be simple Java and OOP over again. Wrong. There were lots of assignments and the goddamn ‘ZyBooks’, which I never cared to do. There was Big O notation, Time/Space Complexity, search algorithms, etc. I remember learning in lecture about how Bill Gates designed his school's scheduling software and supposedly tweaked it, so he'd be placed in classes with more girls. I was excited to take more CS classes.

December rolled around and we sat in the basement lecture hall of Packard for our final exam. I went back into my email to try and find the final exam instructions to see if they mentioned anything about AI. They didn’t. But the final line in my professor’s email made me laugh out loud.

I broke the email up for ease of reading here, but it went like:

“As a reminder, our final exam will take place from 4-7 pm today in Packard Lab 101. Please bring your computers (fully charged) … The exam will include a programming portion… short comprehension questions… Good luck as you wrap up your studying -- you are all amazing humans and can will crush this exam!” – Prof Z

As we sat down for our exams I had been hearing about a tool that could help solve the programming assignments. I had never used it for any of the coding assignments – it was enjoyable to code without it. But the final was difficult. I had decided not to study – the youthful bravado of thinking I knew everything and was the best programmer from my public high school. I raced through the comprehension questions and built two projects that passed the required tests. The next two projects were difficult, however. My code was broken, riddled with errors, and I was starting to panic.

And that, was when I opened Altman’s box. Pause. Pandora’s box. I opened Pandora’s box.

I just threw the code in, it took a while, but I was stumped anyway. 5 or 10 minutes passed, and it gave me an output. I don’t even remember what the output formatting looked like. I threw it in and ran the code. Correct output. Weird. I may have ran a couple of other System.out.println tests throughout the project at certain checkpoints I had struggled with, or I might’ve just instantly clicked submit, closed my laptop, checked my shoulders, the clock and then slowly opened my laptop to pretend that I was still working on it. Yeah, that’s what I probably did.

Maybe my professor was right in her cheery words of motivation, I was an amazing human. I was going to score highly on my first final exam.

(If you look closely at her email, she also had a typo – ‘ and can will crush this exam’. Such a typo would never exist nowadays.)

But I had cheated, right? I decided not to tell anyone about what I did.

December passed along with its holidays, and I hadn’t thought about it very much. Despite failing to reconnect with the high school ex-girlfriend, I had spent the holidays in a good mood and excited to get back to an amazing freshmen year where I’d be rushing a fraternity I’d put in a lot of work to get connected with during the Fall.

When I returned to school to take CSE109, the school had begun issuing statements about AI. An email linking to a LinkedIn post from Nathan Urban Provost and SVP at Lehigh University. linked me to Professor Jeremey Littau’s – one of my older sister (a Lehigh grad and journalism major’s most beloved professors) post on substack. I have officially signed up for substack now. I read through it and parts of my brain in which I’ve been thinking what seems now like primitive thoughts were inspired and brought into a colorful light by Littau’s vivid descriptions. I’ve been thinking for a while now about what our place is once AGI comes to fruition, which ‘experts’ say will be an overnight snap of a finger – akin to Thanos. Littau writes in December of 2022, “My theory on a lot of this generative AI text right now is that its usefulness (learning, thinking, growing from what you read) is correlated with our ability to critique it and ask it follow-up questions.”

He speaks of shaping and prodding the system, like a potter shaping clay. The guide shaping as we see in the public. He says that the things that have finality don’t expand our minds as much.

In my mind, Littau’s theory is no longer that – it is a fact. I was attempting to help install software tools linked to ChatGPT for my mom the other day. A simple google drive add-on in order to prompt the auto-population of google sheets. It had taken me no time at all to do this on my own ChatGPT window. But as I sat down to explain this problem to her window, I found myself extremely frustrated that it didn’t understand my queries as clearly as my window had. So I tried to reframe it as if I were describing the installation and usage to my mom instead of to my personal intelligent assistant. And eventually I got there. Prodding, shaping the system, as Littau writes. I taught my mom the workflow required to prompt GPT to manipulate a google sheet – go into google sheets yourself, create a sheet, copy the link, go to the + sign by the chat box, go to more, and add the drive app to the current chat. Then input the google sheets link, and tell it what you want. I want x ___ so that x. This language, although simple, is powerful in prompting. It’s a simple formula I ___, want x, so that x. But sometimes it’s hard to fill out. I have my own theory - deviating from this formula, or a close relative to it, i.e. mindlessly prompting, promotes a relationship with your “chatbot/ai/agent” whatever you want to call it, that will spin you in loops and assume you are a bumbling idiot who doesn’t know what they want. So yes, prod and shape the system, but be as intentional as possible.

It comes from a project management SCRUM system. After some shallow digging I assume it comes from this page from Dan North.

At the end of his piece, Littau writes in a positive vein about ChatGPT. He writes that learning is asking the follow-up not the initial question. He highlights the strength of using ChatGPT to find that initial starting point more easily. He writes that it’s a “huge opportunity to spend our brainpower on pursuing novel questions of substance and importance.”

A “jumping off point … for truth and real answers”

The end of his piece is powerful and inspiring in the face of the dystopian nihilistic ruin I feel impending when I think about what the human being’s purpose is post “AGI”..

Note: This is my first piece, and I didn’t study writing or journalism at all in college so I know it’s rough, but I’ll work on it!
