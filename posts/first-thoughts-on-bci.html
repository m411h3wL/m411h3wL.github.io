First Thoughts on Brain-Computer Interfaces

June 20, 2026 6 min read

I want to write this down before I know what I think — while it's still genuinely an open question and not a career decision I'm rationalizing after the fact. The question is whether to point the next several years at brain-computer interfaces. Not as a hobby interest, as an actual track: PhD, lab, industry, the whole commitment. I haven't decided. This is me trying to find out why I'm even tempted.

Where this is coming from

It's not coming from nowhere. Most of my research right now is on the engineering side of healthcare access — I lead a project building low-cost biosensing electrodes, the kind that pick up an ECG signal off the skin. That's a different problem from reading a brain, but it's the same family of problem: getting a clean, reliable signal off a body using cheap materials, and making the electronics around that signal trustworthy. A lot of the hard parts of BCI work — electrode-tissue contact, noise, signal drift, the gap between what works in a lab and what survives on an actual person — are hard parts I already spend my days on, just one tissue layer removed.

The other half is the clinical side. I trained as an EMT, which mostly teaches you how much of an emergency is the nervous system failing in some way — stroke, trauma, seizure. And I spent a stretch as a clinical engineering intern at a rehab hospital, which is the slower, longer version of the same lesson: watching people relearn how to do things their nervous system used to handle without being asked. BCI is the most direct engineering answer to that problem that exists. For people with ALS, locked-in syndrome, or spinal cord injury, it's not a convenience feature — it's the only proposed path back to communication or movement that doesn't require the nervous system itself to heal.

What's actually pulling me

I think it's that BCI sits at the exact intersection I already work in — signal acquisition, device design, and a patient who needs the thing to actually work outside a lab — except the stakes and the difficulty are both turned up. ECG electrodes are forgiving. If mine drifts a little, you re-paste it. A BCI that drifts is the difference between a patient communicating and not. That's a worse position to design from, and also a more honest test of whether the engineering is actually good.

There's also a more personal thread: the low-cost, agricultural-surplus electrode work I do now exists because the standard version of that device is priced out of reach for a lot of the world. Almost everything I've read about BCI is the opposite — bleeding-edge, expensive, implanted, built for a handful of research hospitals. If I went into this field, the question I'd actually want to spend a career on isn't "can we read the brain," which increasingly looks like yes. It's "can we read it for cheap enough, and safely enough, that it reaches the people who need it and not just the people who can afford a research trial." That's a less glamorous version of the field, and it's the version I'd actually be useful in.

What's making me hesitate

A few real things, in no particular order:

  • The timeline. This is a research field before it's an industry. Realistically that means a PhD, not a job posting, and I'd be choosing that path over the more direct clinical route I've also been training toward.
  • The ethics aren't solved, and I don't think they're fully solvable in advance. Reading neural signals is a different category of data than a heart rhythm. Consent, ownership of that data, and what happens when the device is implanted and the company that made it isn't around anymore — these aren't edge cases, they're the center of the field, and I don't have answers yet.
  • I'm not sure which seat I want. Treating patients, building the device, or running the research that connects the two are three different careers that all touch this problem, and "interested in BCI" doesn't tell me which one is actually mine.

Why write this now, instead of after deciding

Because I think the decision will be more honest if the reasoning is on the record before I've committed to it. It's easy to retroactively build a clean story for a choice you've already made. It's harder to fake a question you're asking in public before you know the answer. So: this is the question, as of right now. I'll write the answer when I have one — and if I end up somewhere else entirely, I'd rather that be visible too.
